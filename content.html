Complex numbers extend the familiar system of real numbers (integers, fractions, and decimals) by introducing the concept of imaginary numbers. They allow us to represent solutions to equations that have no real number solutions, such as finding the square root of a negative number.

The Imaginary Unit

The foundation of complex numbers is the imaginary unit, denoted by the symbol i (or sometimes j in engineering contexts). It is defined by the equation:

$$i^2 = -1$$

This means that $i$ is the square root of $-1$: $i = \sqrt{-1}$. While this may seem strange (no real number, when squared, gives -1), imaginary numbers are just as valid a mathematical concept as real numbers, and they have many practical applications in physics, engineering, and other fields.

Definition and Representation

A complex number is any number that can be written in the form:

$a + bi$

where $a$ and $b$ are real numbers, and $i$ is the imaginary unit. The number $a$ is called the real part, and $b$ is called the imaginary part (or more precisely, the coefficient of the imaginary unit).

Examples:

  • $3 + 2i$ is a complex number with real part 3 and imaginary part 2
  • $-1 + 4i$ is a complex number with real part -1 and imaginary part 4
  • $5 + 0i = 5$ is a complex number (it's purely real)
  • $0 + 3i = 3i$ is a complex number (it's purely imaginary)
  • $0 + 0i = 0$ is a complex number (it's zero)

Notice that real numbers are a special case of complex numbers where the imaginary part is zero, and purely imaginary numbers are complex numbers where the real part is zero.
